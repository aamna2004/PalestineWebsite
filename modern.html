Modern Era


Following the Ottoman Empire’s defeat in World War I, its territories were divided among European colonial powers. Palestine and Jordan came under British mandate in 1922, while Syria and Lebanon fell to France. Classified as a “Class A” mandate, Palestine was deemed to have an advanced enough infrastructure for provisional independence, though full independence was not achieved. The British mandate supported Zionist aspirations, guided in part by the 1917 Balfour Declaration, which advocated for a “national home for the Jewish people” in Palestine.

Backed by British support, the Zionist movement intensified settlement efforts, forming the Yishuv—a self-governing Jewish community within Palestine. This period saw rising tensions as British policies favored Zionist expansion while Palestinian resistance was suppressed. By the end of the mandate, many Palestinian communities faced displacement, leading to the Nakba in 1948. This period marks the foundation of Israel and a significant upheaval in Palestinian society, the impacts of which continue today.
