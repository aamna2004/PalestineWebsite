Islamic Rule Period


Following the 1516 Ottoman victory over the Mamluks, Palestine became part of the Ottoman Empire, marking a rule that would last over 400 years. Jerusalem held special significance for the Ottomans, inspiring grand construction projects like the iconic city walls built by Suleiman the Magnificent. By 1887, Palestine was organized into three districts, or Sanjaks: Jerusalem, Nablus, and Acre. Jerusalem’s Sanjak, of particular importance, was governed directly by Constantinople. The region’s population of approximately 600,000 was predominantly Sunni Muslim, with Palestinian Christians and a small Jewish community centered in cities like Jerusalem and Hebron.

Relations among the religious communities were generally peaceful, fostered by centuries of shared history. The Ottomans respected this coexistence, as symbolized by inscriptions at Jerusalem’s Jaffa Gate and the tradition of Muslim guardianship over the Holy Sepulcher’s keys. Though tensions arose, especially with efforts to "Turkify" the empire, Ottoman rule ended with World War I, paving the way for new struggles for independence amidst foreign control.
